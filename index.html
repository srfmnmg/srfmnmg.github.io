The Systems Research Foundation

The Systems Research Foundation is a non-profit organization dedicated to pure research in the field of computer systems, networking and other electronic devices. Presently, we are studying the areas of microcomputer operating systems of various hardware architectures and the networking of these machines. We are continually researching into the effective use of older microcomputer hardware in today's networked and multi-user environments.

Our inventory consists of 12 microcomputers of both x86 (IA-32) and PowerPC architectures. We aim to augment this inventory with Alpha and SPARC acquisitions in the future. The operating systems currently used within the Systems Research Foundation are Linux, OpenBSD, Mac OS and Windows. In the past we have also used FreeBSD and NetBSD, in addition to having supplemental experience on Solaris, MPE/XL, HP-UX and OpenVMS.

The Systems Research Foundation was founded in 1998 on the combined efforts of MN Software Consulting and Theoretical Networking Research to install the Linux operating system on an IBM PS/2 model 56slc with a 20 MHz Intel 80386 processor, 4 MB of main memory, an on-board SCSI controller (with klunker IBM SCSI hard disks) and an MCA 3Com network interface card dug out of the Information Management "computer graveyard". After about four-plus hours of work on this machine, Slackware Linux was finally installed, and the computer was up and communicating with the college network. This first networked, multi-user system was affectionately named "iconoclast" in homage to Emerson, Lake and Palmer's "Tarkus" album. From that day forward, it has been a major tenet of The Systems Research Foundation that older computer hardware is not necessarily "paperweight material", as many might believe.

PS/2 and Micro-Channel links of interest

IBM PS/2 systems with the Micro-Channel Architecture (MCA) bus are near and dear to our hearts. Although they have caused great amounts of annoyance, headaches and hand-wringing in our own personal experience (as in trying to get three 300 MiB SCSI hard disks to all work together), if you finally do wrestle the beasts into submission, you feel like you've really accomplished something. (Even if all you have to show for it is a relatively slow 386 or 486 GNU/Linux system!) I still personally find it amazing that we were ever able to get these systems going at all.
